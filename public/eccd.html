ECCD IN CAMBODIA

Previous Next


The Cambodian government has been committed to ECCD for years and has adopted framework laws, drafted several national policies and action plans, which have been implemented through different programmes, but many difficulties and challenges remain.



Historical Milestones

“State Preschools” were the first educational services provided to young children across the country. State Preschool Teachers have to pass a two-year pre-service training. However, due to budgetary and human capital constraints, the provision of state preschools is limited and concentrated around urban and peri-urban areas. Hence, the government chose to invest in cost-effective alternatives in order to reach a greater number of children: “Community-based preschools”. This model was first developped in 1997 by the NGOs Planète Enfants & Développement (PE&D), Plan International and Save the Children, with support from the UNICEF, then scaled up nationwide in the following years. In 2019, there were 2 970 community preschools in Cambodia, reaching 69 914 children, including 36 073 girls. Teaching is usually conducted by a community member, for 98% female, who has received a shorter training than state-preschool teachers. Pedagogy is similar to the one promoted within state preschools but the school environment differs, as there is often no proper facility (teaching is conducted within the pagoda compound or at a villager’s home). In order to be standardized by the MoEYS and benefit from nearly similar funding than State preschools, CPS must meet minimum standards defined by the sub-decree on CPS management.


Legal Framework

Education is an integral part of Early Childhood Care and Development. In Cambodia, this right is protected by the Constitution. Article 48: “the State shall assure the protection of children’s rights as enshrined in the Convention on Children, especially, the right to life, the right to education, the right to protection during wartime and the right to protection from economic or sexual exploitation”. Early childcare and childhood education are defined in the law (Royal Kram No. NS/RKM/1207/032 on Education, 08 December 2007, art 16-17) as services provided for children aged between 0 and 6 years old. Early childhood education is not a level of education but constitutes a preparation for the schooling period.


Main Challenges

According to UNICEF, since 2007, the number of children enrolled in preschool programmes has more than doubled. However, despite the considerable progress that has been made in education in Cambodia, many challenges remain. Only 27% of 3-5 year olds are developmentally appropriate in literacy and numeracy, and by the age of 17, 55% of adolescents will have dropped out of school (UNICEF).

The main challenges for ECCD in Cambodia are the following:

• Infrastructures: poor quality of learning environments, some places are not suitable for school, and lack of basic infrastructure, such as water, sanitation and hygiene (WASH) facilities
• Lack of qualified teachers
• Quality: the quality of preschools in Cambodia is uneven, particularly in terms of teaching and learning, due to a lack of resources and support
• Parents’ unwillingness to send their children: many don’t understand the value of education and most cannot afford to send their children to school, particularly in rural and deprived areas. Gross enrollment rate for children age from 3 to 5 years in preschool is only 43% (UNICEF).
• 90% of the poor live in rural areas, where there is a lack of State preschools
• 60% of Community-based Preschools are functioning below minimum standards.
